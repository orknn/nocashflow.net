What Is Smart Money, and Where Is It Going?

NoCashFlow · Apr 6, 2026

In January, JPMorgan said institutional investors would dominate crypto flows in 2026. But the Q1 numbers came in the opposite. The Iran war, VIX at 26, Fear & Greed at 12 — smart money retreated.

Q1 2026 Institutional Crypto Flow
$11B realized vs. $33B projected — −67% miss

The Coinbase Premium Index shows exactly this. As institutions pulled out of spot Bitcoin, their derivatives positions turned negative too. Smart money got scared. But is this temporary?

"Smart money doesn't leave markets — it rotates. The question is not where it went, but where it's going next."

What Does the Coinbase Premium Index Say?

Coinbase Premium = Coinbase BTC price − Binance BTC price. A positive spread = institutional buying pressure (US institutions buy on Coinbase). A negative spread = retail-dominated or institutional outflow.

  • Feb 2026: the premium fell to zero — institutional flow stopped
  • Mar 2026: turned negative — the first time since the FTX collapse
  • April: minimal recovery. The war premium is in oil, not in crypto.

#02 · Commodities · Mar 23, 2026

Dr. Copper — Wrong Diagnosis?

Copper has long been the economy's thermometer. But AI data centers, EVs and renewables now demand it at once. S&P Global projects a 10M-tonne deficit by 2040.

2024–2026 data breaks the "Dr. Copper" relationship: copper stays elevated while PMI contracts. Electrification demand has overtaken cyclical demand.

  • EV: one electric car = 4× the copper of a conventional car
  • Solar: 1 GW = 1,000 tonnes of copper
  • Supply: the average mine takes 17 years to develop

#01 · Macro · Mar 16, 2026

Why the 1970s Comparison Is Misleading

Brent up 40%, Hormuz closed — everyone is drawing 1970s analogies. But in 1973 it took 1 barrel per $1,000 of GDP; today, 0.43. PPI came in double expectations. Powell said "we don't know" 17 times.

Oil Intensity Comparison
1973: 1.0 barrel/$1,000 GDP → 2026: 0.43 barrel/$1,000 GDP

The same price shock does damage of a different magnitude today. But the second-round effects (energy → producer → consumer prices) haven't fully shown up yet.

"The 1970s is the wrong analogy — but that doesn't mean this is painless." — Gita Gopinath, IMF
